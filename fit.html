Why Fit Is Scored Like the Case
Candidates underprepare for fit — that's exactly where you can pull ahead
The honest truth about fit
The interviewer's real question is simple: would I want this person on my team at a client site at 11pm? Cases test your IQ; fit tests your EQ — communication, poise, self-awareness, and whether your stories hold up. The interviewer is assessing these qualities across both portions of the interview, not just the fit block.

Consulting is a highly interpersonal business, so firms genuinely care about this section. It's also the part you can control most: unlike a case, you know the questions in advance. Prepared, well-structured stories are one of the easiest ways to look ready. If you win the interviewer over during fit, the case tends to go more smoothly — they already want you to succeed.

Show, don't tell
Answer every behavioural question with a specific story, not a self-description. "I'm a strong leader" is telling. A 90-second story where your action changed the outcome is showing — and it's what interviewers remember.
1
Three Story Structures
Pick the one that fits your natural style — then use it consistently

All three do the same job: keep your story tight, logical, and easy to follow. The differences are small. Learn one well rather than switching between them mid-interview.

STAR
Wharton casebook
SSituation — set up the problem or context
TTask — what you were responsible for
AAction — what you specifically did
RResult — the outcome, impact, and what you learned
SCARF
Wharton casebook
SSituation — context and background
CComplication — the tension or conflict
AAction — what you did about it
RResult — the outcome and impact
FFuture lessons — what you'd carry forward
H-SOAR-L
Chicago Booth casebook
HHigh-level overview — a one-line "headline" of the story and your role
SSituation — context, in a sentence or two
OObstacle — the problem to be resolved
AAction — spend most of your time here
RResult — outcome and impact on team / client
LLearning — what the experience taught you
Lead with the headline
Open with a "newspaper headline" sentence that tells the interviewer where the story is going, then fill in the details. This lets them follow your logic from the first sentence — the same answer-first habit that scores well in the case.
2
Timing & Time-Weighting
Where the seconds should actually go

Aim for a full story in roughly 90 to 120 seconds — long enough to show depth, short enough to stay sharp. The most common mistake is spending too long on setup and too little on what you actually did. Weight your time like this:

Headline~15%
Situation~15%
Action~55%
Result~15%
Why action gets the majority
The interviewer is scoring your judgement and behaviour, not the situation. Minimise time on context and obstacle; spend the bulk describing the specific actions you took and the decisions you made. Use "I" more than "we" — the team's win is nice, but your individual contribution is what's being assessed.
3
The Six Question Categories
Nearly every behavioural question maps to one of these — prepare a story for each
1
Individual Contribution
Tell me about your proudest accomplishment.
What are your top two strengths and two weaknesses?
You set an ambitious goal — how did you achieve it?
What's the biggest asset you'd bring to a project team?
2
Leadership / Management
A time you made an unpopular decision.
A time you managed a difficult team member or client.
You led a team through a difficult situation.
A time you gave someone negative feedback.
3
Persuasion / Influence
You convinced someone with an opposing viewpoint.
A disagreement with your manager.
How do you get buy-in from stakeholders?
The best presentation you've delivered.
4
Analytics / Problem Solving
A highly analytical project you worked on.
You used data and analytics to solve a problem.
A time you had to simplify complex technical data.
You used data to drive a conclusion — what did you assume?
5
Challenge / Failure
A time you overcame a significant obstacle.
You were wrong and someone had to convince you.
You navigated a hard situation under time pressure.
A time you failed to meet a deadline or goal.
6
Teamwork / Collaboration
A time you worked in a diverse or cross-cultural team.
You resolved conflict between teammates.
You supported a struggling colleague.
A time you had to compromise to move the team forward.
One story can cover several categories
A single strong story — say, turning around a stalled founder project — can answer leadership, persuasion, and challenge prompts with small reframing. You don't need 24 stories; you need 5–8 flexible ones you can angle toward the question asked.
4
"Why Consulting" & "Why This Firm"
These aren't story questions — they need a different, tighter answer

Resume walk-throughs and motivation questions don't need a story structure — keep them crisp and rehearsed. Three you should have polished cold:

  • 1
    Walk me through your resume. A logical narrative: each move should explain the rationale behind the decision, ending with why consulting is the natural next step. Not a list of jobs — a through-line.
  • 2
    Why consulting? Tie it to your career progression and long-term goals. For a founder, the honest, strong version is usually about breadth of exposure, pattern recognition across industries, and building an operator's toolkit at pace.
  • 3
    Why this firm / this office? Give specific reasons — a practice area, the regional platform (e.g. the firm's Middle East / GCC presence), people you've spoken to. Generic answers that fit any firm read as unprepared.
Make the networking count
The strongest "why firm" answers name real conversations: "When I spoke with a partner in the Dubai office about their public-sector transformation work…" Specificity here is impossible to fake and signals genuine interest.
5
Build Your Story Bank
A single sheet mapping 5–8 stories to the qualities they demonstrate

Keep a simple sheet with your top 5–8 stories, each written as a four-to-five-sentence outline in your chosen structure. Tag each with the qualities and categories it can cover, so in the interview you can instantly reach for the right one. Draft skeleton below — fill the last column with your own founder and MBA experiences (global / GCC framing):

Cover a range, not repeats
Make sure your set spans different situations and skills — leadership, teamwork, a difficult client, use of data, a genuine failure. If three of your stories are all "I led a team to a win," you have a gap the interviewer will find.
6
Four Mistakes That Sink Fit Answers
Avoid these and you're already ahead of most candidates
  • 1
    Telling instead of showing. "I'm collaborative" scores nothing. A specific story where your action produced a result scores everything.
  • 2
    Drowning in setup. Two minutes of context and ten seconds of action. Flip it — headline fast, then spend your time on what you did.
  • 3
    Hiding behind "we". The team's success is fine, but the interviewer is scoring you. Be explicit about your individual contribution.
  • 4
    Generic "why firm" answers. An answer that fits every firm equally reads as unprepared. Name the practice, the office, the people, the regional platform.
A quiet failure signal
Rambling and unstructured answers hurt you even when the content is good — because structure and concision are exactly the client-readiness skills the fit interview is designed to test.

Quick Reference — Fit Checklist
Read this the morning of the interview
Fit Interview Standards
1Open with a one-line headline so the interviewer knows where the story is going.
2Target 90–120 seconds per story — depth without rambling.
3Spend ~55% of the time on your actions; keep situation and obstacle brief.
4Say "I" more than "we" — your individual contribution is what's scored.
5Show, don't tell: every claim about yourself backed by a specific story.
6Close with the result and what you learned — impact plus self-awareness.
7Have "why consulting / why firm / why office" polished cold, with specifics.
8Bring 5–8 flexible stories that span leadership, teamwork, data, and a real failure.
